You've heard about AI tools for genealogy. Maybe you've even tried them. But a question lingers: is it safe to trust artificial intelligence with your family history?

This concern makes sense. Your family's story matters. You don't want to introduce errors or rely on technology that might mislead you. The headlines about AI "hallucinations" don't help. Neither does the hype that makes AI sound like magic.

Here's the truth: AI is a tool. Like any tool, it can be used well or poorly. The safety depends on how you use it.

## What "Safe" Means for Genealogy

When family historians ask if AI is safe, they usually mean several things.

**Will it make up information?** Yes, sometimes. AI can generate plausible-sounding details that aren't true. This is the biggest risk and the one requiring the most attention.

**Will it steal my research?** This depends on the tool and your settings. Most major AI tools don't permanently store or share your conversations by default. But reading privacy policies matters.

**Will it replace real research?** No. AI can't access most genealogy databases. It can't read original documents in archives. It can't interview living relatives. The core work remains yours.

**Will it make me lazy?** Only if you let it. AI can enhance your thinking or substitute for it. The choice is yours.

Understanding these concerns helps you use AI wisely rather than avoiding it entirely.

## Where AI Helps Genealogy Research

AI tools excel at certain tasks that frustrate human researchers.

### Brainstorming and Strategy

When you're stuck, AI can suggest approaches you haven't considered. Ask it where to find records for a specific time and place. Ask what historical events might explain a family pattern. Ask what other researchers have tried with similar problems.

AI draws on vast training data that includes genealogical methods. It won't always be right, but it often sparks useful directions.

### Reading Difficult Handwriting

Some AI tools can help transcribe old documents. Upload an image of that impossible German script or faded census page. The AI might decode what your eyes can't.

This isn't foolproof. Always verify transcriptions against the original. But AI can give you a starting point when you're staring at squiggles.

### Understanding Historical Context

AI knows a lot about history. Ask it to explain the Homestead Act, or describe daily life in 1890s New York tenements, or outline the waves of German immigration.

This context helps you understand your ancestors' choices. It transforms names and dates into lived experience.

### Drafting and Writing

This is where AI shines for family historians. Many researchers struggle to turn their findings into readable stories. AI can help draft paragraphs, suggest structures, and overcome writer's block.

You provide the facts and the family knowledge. AI helps with the prose. The result is still your story, just written more easily.

### Organizing Information

AI can help structure your research. Describe what you've found and ask it to identify gaps. Paste in messy notes and ask it to organize them. Use it as a thinking partner for making sense of complexity.

## Where AI Falls Short

Knowing AI's limits protects you from its risks.

### AI Cannot Access Genealogy Databases

ChatGPT and Claude don't have accounts on Ancestry, FamilySearch, or FindMyPast. They can't search records for you. They can't verify what they say against actual sources.

When AI makes specific claims about your ancestors, be suspicious. Unless you provided that information, the AI is likely inventing details.

### AI Hallucinates with Confidence

AI doesn't know what it doesn't know. It will generate plausible names, dates, and places that don't exist. It won't tell you it's generating answers, but that is what it is doing.

I've seen AI invent entire family lines that sounded reasonable but had no basis in reality. The confident tone makes it easy to be fooled. Always verify AI-generated facts against primary sources.

### AI Reflects Its Training Data

AI learned from the internet, which contains errors, biases, and outdated information. It may repeat genealogical myths. It may reflect historical bias. 

Your critical thinking remains essential. AI can make mistakes.

### AI Can't Replace Human Judgment

Family history involves interpretation. Why did grandma never talk about her father? What do we make of conflicting records? How do we write about difficult ancestors?

These questions require human wisdom. AI can offer perspectives, but the judgment is yours.

## How to Use AI Safely

Safe AI use comes down to habits and awareness.

**Verify everything.** Treat AI output as a hypothesis to test, not a fact to trust. Check claims against primary sources before including them in your research.

**Provide context.** AI works better when you tell it what you already know. Upload those research notes and transcriptions or just type it in the chat. "My ancestor John Smith lived in Lancaster County, Pennsylvania in the 1850s" gets better results than just asking about "John Smith."

**Use it for process, not facts.** AI excels at helping you think, organize, and write. It's weaker at providing accurate historical details. Play to its strengths.

**Keep your sources.** Don't let AI replace your documentation habits. Every fact in your family history should trace back to a source you've evaluated.

**Protect sensitive information.** Be thoughtful about sharing living relatives' personal details with AI tools. Read privacy policies. Use settings that limit data retention when available.

**Stay current.** AI tools improve fast. What was true about their capabilities six months ago may not be true today. Keep learning. The Chronicle Makers community keeps up-to-date on all these tools.

## The Real Risk Is Not Using AI

Here's what I've come to believe: the bigger risk isn't using AI poorly. It's not using it at all.

Family historians who embrace these tools finish more projects. They write more stories. They break through more brick walls. They enjoy the work more because the tedious parts become easier.

Those who avoid AI out of fear miss these benefits. Their research stays in boxes. Their stories stay unwritten.

Family historians who use AI are accomplishing 10-20x more than those who don't.

## Getting Started Safely

If you're new to AI for genealogy, start small.

Pick one simple task. Maybe drafting a paragraph about an ancestor you know well. Maybe asking for historical context on a place your family lived.

See how the tool responds. Notice where it's helpful and where it falls short. Build your understanding through experience rather than fear.
